Why age-gating matters legally and ethically

Age verification on adult platforms serves two distinct purposes that are often conflated:

  • Legal compliance: Several categories of content and services are regulated based on user age. Wagering, alcohol, adult content, and certain social platforms have statutory age requirements in the US and internationally. Platforms that knowingly allow underage users to access regulated services face liability.
  • Ethical responsibility: Separate from legal requirements, allowing minors on platforms designed for adults creates potential harm. Adults-only platforms have content, language, and interaction norms that are inappropriate for minors.

The two purposes sometimes align and sometimes don't. A platform may be legally compliant with age verification requirements while still falling short of what ethical responsibility requires, or vice versa.

Verification methods: soft to hard

Age declaration (checkbox / DOB entry)

The weakest form. Users type in a birth year or check a box asserting they're 18+. No verification is performed. Any minor who knows to type a different year can pass. This is compliance theater—it creates a record that the user declared their age but does not verify it.

Email + account registration

Slightly stronger. An email address is associated with the account. If the account is banned, the email is banned (though users can create new emails easily). Still no age verification.

Phone number verification

A verification code sent to a phone number creates one additional friction layer. Phone numbers are more constrained than email addresses, but minors easily access phones. Not age verification in any meaningful sense.

Payment card requirement

Payment cards are nominally adult-linked (requiring an 18+ bank account or credit application). This creates meaningful friction. Minors can still access parents' cards, but the economic barrier to mass bot farms and most casual bad actors is real. This is where most "hard" verification starts in practice.

ID document upload

Users upload a government-issued ID. The platform or a third-party service reviews and confirms the ID. This is genuine age verification—it actually confirms the user is who they say they are and that they're of legal age. Trade-offs: storage of sensitive identity documents creates privacy risk; friction reduces sign-up conversion significantly.

Third-party identity verification (Stripe Identity, Persona, etc.)

Users verify age through a third-party service. The service confirms verification status to the platform without the platform storing the actual ID document. This is the current best-practice approach—combines verification strength with reduced platform liability for document storage.

Payment as a proxy for age verification

The payment card approach is worth examining carefully because it's widely used and often misunderstood:

Why it helps: Credit and debit cards require applicants to be 18+ (or 16+ for some debit cards in some jurisdictions). The card application involves identity checks. A card-required account is harder to create than an anonymous one.

Why it's not complete: Minors regularly use parents' cards. Prepaid cards can be purchased for cash. The payment check verifies that a payment method exists, not that the person creating the account is the same person who created the payment method.

Payment friction is best understood as one layer in a multi-layer approach, not as age verification on its own.

Biometric and AI approaches

Emerging approaches to age verification use visual analysis:

  • Facial age estimation: AI models estimate a user's age from a webcam photo or uploaded selfie. Accuracy is improving but still imperfect, especially at the 18–25 boundary where estimation variance is highest.
  • Liveness detection + age estimation: Combining liveness detection (confirming a live person is present, not a photo) with age estimation improves accuracy. Used in some financial and regulatory contexts.
  • Behavioral biometrics: Typing cadence, mouse movement, and interaction patterns can statistically predict age ranges. Used as supplemental signal rather than standalone verification.

These approaches are improving rapidly but are not yet precise enough to serve as standalone age verification for regulated services.

US legal landscape

The US framework involves federal law and an increasingly active state-level landscape:

  • COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act): Federal law requiring parental consent before collecting personal data from children under 13. Applies to platforms directed at children.
  • State age verification laws: Multiple states (Texas, Louisiana, Arkansas, others) have passed laws requiring age verification for online adult content or social media platforms. Enforcement and scope vary. These are being challenged and modified through litigation.
  • FTC enforcement: The FTC has authority to pursue platforms that fail to adequately protect minors from harm. This extends beyond COPPA to general consumer protection standards.
  • KOSA (Kids Online Safety Act): Federal legislation (status varies) that would impose broader safety requirements on platforms likely to be used by minors.

What responsible platforms do

A responsible adult platform:

  • Uses at minimum payment-based friction combined with terms-of-service age declaration
  • For regulated activities (wagering, adult content), implements ID-document or third-party verification
  • Does not market to or engage minors in any way
  • Maintains a clear, accessible process for reporting suspected minors
  • Has a clear policy and process for what happens when a minor is discovered on the platform
  • Keeps privacy implications of verification in balance—collecting only what's needed for the compliance purpose

Limitations and reality check

No verification system is perfect. Determined individuals can defeat any mechanism:

  • ID documents can be forged (harder than it sounds, but possible)
  • Minors can use parents' verified accounts
  • Account sharing can move verified access to unverified users

Verification systems are designed to make non-compliant access costly enough that most attempts don't succeed and most would-be violators go elsewhere. They are statistical controls, not absolute barriers. Platforms that invest in verification are meaningfully safer than those that don't, while acknowledging that no system is 100% effective.

FAQ

Why do some platforms say "18+" but clearly have minors on them?

Because their verification is declarative, not substantive. A checkbox or date-of-birth entry doesn't verify anything—it just creates a record that the user said they're 18+. Without a hard verification step, the declaration is unenforceable.

Is age verification a privacy concern?

It can be. Collecting and storing government ID documents creates privacy risk. Third-party verification services that confirm status without the platform storing the document reduce (though don't eliminate) this risk. Users should check a platform's privacy policy for how verification data is handled.
