Warning Signs of Problem Gambling & Where to Get Help

Problem gambling affects millions of Americans and often develops gradually from recreational play. This guide outlines the warning signs clearly, who's at higher risk, and exactly where to go for help—whether it's for yourself or someone you're concerned about.

What problem gambling is (and isn't)

Problem gambling—also called gambling disorder—is a recognised behavioural condition characterised by continued gambling despite harmful consequences and difficulty controlling the behaviour. It sits on a spectrum: from recreational gambling (no problem), through at-risk gambling (developing patterns), to problem gambling (significant negative impact), to severe gambling disorder.

What it is NOT:

  • A moral failure or weakness of character
  • Defined purely by how much money is involved
  • Defined by losing—problem gambling can affect people who are net winners
  • Easily visible from the outside—many problem gamblers are high-functioning in other areas of life

Anyone who wagers can develop gambling disorder given the right conditions. Understanding the warning signs is not about labelling—it's about awareness.

10 warning signs of problem gambling

These signs are adapted from the DSM-5 criteria for gambling disorder and the National Council on Problem Gambling's diagnostic framework. The presence of multiple signs is more significant than any single one.

  1. Preoccupation: Thinking about gambling when you're not playing—planning the next session, reliving past sessions, thinking about how to get more money to gamble with.
  2. Tolerance: Needing to bet more money or play longer to get the same level of excitement.
  3. Failed attempts to stop: Repeated, genuine attempts to cut back or stop gambling that haven't worked.
  4. Restlessness when stopping: Feeling irritable, anxious, or restless when trying to reduce or stop gambling.
  5. Escape: Gambling to escape problems, relieve guilt, anxiety, depression, or helplessness.
  6. Chasing: Returning to gamble after losses with the intent to "win back" the money.
  7. Concealment: Lying to family members, therapists, or others to hide the extent of gambling.
  8. Relationship impact: Gambling jeopardising a significant relationship, job, or educational opportunity.
  9. Financial consequences: Relying on others for money to relieve gambling-related financial problems.
  10. Increasing risk-taking: Escalating bet sizes, playing at higher stakes than intended, or feeling compelled to "go big".

The DSM-5 defines gambling disorder as 4 or more of these criteria in a 12-month period, with significant distress or impairment. However, 2–3 signs still indicate an at-risk pattern worth addressing proactively.

Who's at higher risk

Problem gambling can affect anyone, but certain factors increase vulnerability:

  • Age: Younger adults (18–30) are at higher risk, partly due to higher impulsivity and partly due to greater exposure to online platforms.
  • Mental health history: Depression, anxiety, ADHD, and bipolar disorder are associated with higher gambling disorder rates. Gambling may be used as self-medication for mood regulation.
  • Substance use: Co-occurring alcohol or drug problems significantly increase gambling disorder risk.
  • Family history: Gambling disorder has a heritable component. A family history of addiction increases personal risk.
  • Early wins: A significant early win can create a false belief that skill or luck will continue to favour you, increasing risk tolerance.
  • Social isolation: People with fewer social connections and support networks are more vulnerable to problematic use of any engaging platform.

What to do if you recognise yourself

Recognition is the first step, and it's significant. Here's a practical path forward:

  1. Pause gambling immediately. If you recognise multiple warning signs, stopping while you seek support is the priority. Use a cooling-off period or self-exclusion on any platform you use.
  2. Call the helpline. 1-800-522-4700. You don't need to have a crisis to call. Helpline counsellors are trained to help people at all stages of recognition.
  3. Tell one person. Secrecy makes the problem larger. Telling one trusted person—not to ask for help financially, just to break the isolation—is consistently reported as a turning point by people who've recovered.
  4. Remove access. Self-exclude from platforms, remove apps, tell a household member to change passwords if needed. Environmental barriers work better than willpower alone.
  5. Get professional support. Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) has strong evidence for gambling disorder treatment. Your GP or a licensed therapist can refer you. Some states have free gambling counselling services.

If you're worried about someone else

Watching someone you care about struggle with gambling can be frustrating and frightening. Some practical guidance:

  • Avoid covering financial losses. Paying debts created by gambling removes the natural consequence that sometimes prompts recognition of a problem.
  • Don't give ultimatums in anger. If you set a boundary, only set ones you'll actually maintain. Ultimatums that aren't followed through undermine future credibility.
  • Support from a distance. You can be supportive without enabling. Being available, non-judgmental, and consistent is more helpful than constant confrontation.
  • Use Gam-Anon: A support community specifically for people affected by someone else's problem gambling. Free and confidential.
  • Prioritise your own wellbeing. You cannot fix someone else's problem. Taking care of your own mental health is not selfish—it's necessary.

FAQ

Can someone recover from problem gambling?

Yes. Problem gambling is treatable. Research shows that cognitive behavioural therapy, peer support (like Gamblers Anonymous), and counselling are effective. Many people recover and maintain recovery long-term.

Is online gambling more addictive than in-person gambling?

Online gambling has characteristics that increase risk: accessibility (available 24/7 from home), speed (fast-paced games with rapid feedback), privacy (no social observation), and ease of access. These factors don't make problem gambling inevitable but can accelerate its development in vulnerable individuals.

Can problem gambling be prevented?

Awareness, pre-set limits, honest self-reflection, and choosing platforms with robust responsible gaming tools all reduce risk. Problem gambling, like other behavioural conditions, is rarely triggered by a single decision—it develops through patterns. Interrupting those patterns early is the most effective prevention.
