Online Social Life After COVID: What Changed and What Stayed

COVID forced billions of people to develop entirely new social habits in a matter of weeks. Six years later, most in-person socialising has returned—but the digital habits haven't disappeared. Some of them became permanent.

How COVID rewired social expectations

Before 2020, video calling with people you weren't already close to was mildly awkward. It was a work necessity or a long-distance relationship tool—not a default social format. The idea of joining a video call with strangers for purely social reasons had minimal cultural penetration outside specific gaming communities.

COVID changed this in a few specific ways:

  • Video call normalisation: Hundreds of millions of people adopted video calls for work, family, and social life within a single year. The technical barrier and social awkwardness dropped dramatically through sheer repetition.
  • The camera as social presence: With in-person options removed, camera presence became the only form of face-to-face connection available. People who had avoided cameras for years spent months camera-on daily. The baseline discomfort level dropped significantly.
  • Social time online: Dinner parties, game nights, drinks with friends—all moved online. People discovered that some of this worked better than expected and that the activity mattered more than the medium.
  • Loneliness acceleration: The flip side: people who had few strong social connections before 2020 found digital substitutes inadequate for deep human needs. Loneliness data from 2020–2022 was historically elevated, and the effects have persisted.

The normalisation of camera-first socialising

Pre-pandemic, "stranger danger" logic extended to online video. Meeting someone on a random video chat platform had a fringe connotation. Post-pandemic, the concept of "meeting someone on video first" is genuinely mainstream—Zoom first dates, video coffee chats with potential new friends, and live-streamed community events are all post-COVID normal.

This matters for random video platforms specifically: the user who would have dismissed Omegle or Chatroulette as "weird" in 2019 is significantly more open to the format in 2026, because camera-first stranger interaction has been normalised by three years of pandemic-era practice.

What platforms filled the gap during and after

The pandemic created demand for online social platforms at a scale that existing products couldn't absorb. The winners:

  • Discord: Became the community infrastructure for online social groups across gaming, work, education, and hobbies. Communities that formed on Discord during COVID have largely persisted.
  • Houseparty and equivalents: The pandemic-era video game party format demonstrated genuine demand for multiplayer social gaming—though Houseparty itself was later shut down.
  • Twitch and streaming: Live streaming as participatory entertainment normalised watching strangers and interacting with them in real time.
  • Random video chat (post-Omegle): The post-Omegle landscape of structured random video platforms attracted the wave of adults who wanted the serendipity of stranger encounter without the chaos of unmoderated platforms.

Why random-stranger formats survived

Counter-intuitively, the COVID period was good for random video chat. When you're limited to your own home and social network, the idea of meeting a completely random person with zero shared history becomes appealing rather than strange. The serendipity of an unexpected encounter is scarce when in-person serendipity is gone.

Post-COVID, the format survived because:

  • The user base had expanded to include older, less technically adventurous demographics
  • The normalisation of camera-first interaction lowered the entry barrier
  • The emotional benefit of genuine stranger encounters—novelty, possibility, the randomness itself—remained valuable as in-person social life returned but felt more scripted and familiar
  • New platforms (post-Omegle) added structure that made the format more appealing to adults who found pure roulette too chaotic

Gaming together as the new hanging out

One of the most durable behavioural changes from the pandemic period is the acceptance of gaming as social infrastructure. Before 2020, "hanging out" meant physical presence. COVID forced millions of people to discover that playing games together online produced genuine social connection—sometimes better than passive in-person activities like watching TV.

By 2026, online gaming as a social activity is fully normalised across age groups that it wasn't in 2019. The 35-year-old playing poker online with strangers on Shitbox Shuffle is not unusual—they're using a pattern that COVID helped establish as mainstream adult social behaviour.

Where things stand in 2026

Six years after the pandemic began, the social landscape has some clear features:

  • In-person socialising has recovered, but not at the expense of online. People do both more than before.
  • Camera-first digital interaction is a permanent feature of adult social life, no longer a reluctant substitute.
  • Adult loneliness remains elevated compared to pre-pandemic levels, creating genuine demand for platforms that facilitate real connection.
  • The generations who came of age during or just after the pandemic have entirely camera-normalised social habits—they're the most natural audience for video-first platforms.
  • The market for structured, adult-verified, video-plus-activity platforms (like Shitbox Shuffle) has never been larger.

We're in the early days of figuring out what online social life looks like in the medium-term. What's clear is that it will include video, it will include games, and it will include strangers.
