The 10 poker hands ranked (highest to lowest)

  1. Royal Flush: A, K, Q, J, 10 of the same suit. Rarest hand in the game. Unbeatable.
  2. Straight Flush: Five consecutive cards of the same suit (e.g. 7-8-9-10-J of hearts).
  3. Four of a Kind (Quads): Four cards of the same rank (e.g. four Queens).
  4. Full House: Three of a kind + a pair (e.g. three Kings + two 4s). Ranked by the three-of-a-kind first.
  5. Flush: Any five cards of the same suit, non-sequential. Ranked by highest card.
  6. Straight: Five consecutive cards of mixed suits. Ace can be high (A-K-Q-J-10) or low (A-2-3-4-5).
  7. Three of a Kind (Trips/Set): Three cards of the same rank.
  8. Two Pair: Two separate pairs (e.g. two Aces + two 8s).
  9. One Pair: Two cards of the same rank.
  10. High Card: No combination made. Highest card in hand plays.

Ties are broken by the highest card(s) in the winning combination, then kickers (remaining cards).

Pre-flop starting hand tiers

In Texas Hold'em, you're dealt two hole cards before any community cards are shown. Your decision whether to fold, call, or raise before the flop is one of the most important in the hand. Here's a rough tier system:

Tier 1 — Premium hands (almost always play)

  • AA, KK, QQ (pocket pairs)
  • AK suited (AKs)
  • JJ

Tier 2 — Strong hands (usually play)

  • TT, 99, 88
  • AQ suited, AK offsuit, AJ suited
  • KQ suited

Tier 3 — Playable (position and context dependent)

  • 77, 66, 55
  • AQ offsuit, AJ offsuit, KQ offsuit
  • Suited connectors (78s, 89s, 9Ts) — best in later position

Tier 4 — Marginal (fold in most situations)

  • Low pocket pairs (22–44) without a clear edge
  • Hands like K8 offsuit, Q7 suited—too many better hands exist

Position matters enormously in Hold'em. The later you act (closer to the dealer button), the more information you have when making decisions. Hands that aren't worth playing in early position become playable in late position.

Reading the board

The five community cards (flop, turn, river) interact with your hole cards to make the best possible 5-card hand. Novice players often miss board texture—what the community cards say about what other players might have.

Key board-reading habits:

  • Flush draws: If three or more cards on the board share a suit, any player holding two cards of that suit has a flush. Bet or call accordingly.
  • Straight draws: Connected board cards (e.g. 6-7-8) make straights possible for multiple holdings. Be aware of what completes them on the turn and river.
  • Paired boards: If the board shows a pair (e.g. K-K-7), full houses and quads become possible. Strong hands get stronger, but trips and two pairs that seemed good become vulnerable.
  • Dry boards: Disconnected, low-card boards (e.g. 2-7-Q offsuit) are good for continuation-betting because few opponents will have hit anything meaningful.

Pot odds simplified

Pot odds tell you whether calling a bet is mathematically profitable given your probability of winning. The short version:

Pot odds = amount to call ÷ (pot size + amount to call)

If the pot is 100 and you need to call 25, your pot odds are 25/125 = 20%. If your probability of making your winning hand is greater than 20%, calling is profitable. If it's less, fold.

Common drawing probabilities to remember:

  • Open-ended straight draw (8 outs) on the turn: ~17%
  • Flush draw (9 outs) on the turn: ~19%
  • Flush draw with both turn and river to come: ~35%
  • One pair against overcards: ~55% favourite

You don't need exact math at the table—rough approximations are enough. "Outs × 2 = % probability on the turn" is a reliable shortcut.

Bluffing strangers on video

Poker on Shitbox Shuffle includes the video element—your opponent can see you. This changes the bluffing dynamic from online-only poker:

What works against video opponents:

  • Semi-bluffs: Bluffing when you have a draw that can improve to the best hand. You have two ways to win (opponent folds, or you hit your draw). This is more profitable than pure bluffs.
  • Consistent behavior: If you're naturally expressive, don't try to suppress it. Consistency is harder to read than suppression—opponents looking for tells expect tension, not naturalness.
  • Game-based banter: The social layer of video can be used to distract or disarm. Friendly chat mid-hand doesn't reveal your hand, but it does break an opponent's concentration.

What video adds for defence:

  • You can watch for hesitation before a big bet (suggests a draw or weak hand).
  • Expressions after a community card hits often reveal whether it helped them.
  • Overconfident behaviour when the card hits the board can indicate they made their draw.

FAQ

Does a flush beat a straight?

Yes. A flush (five cards of the same suit) ranks above a straight (five consecutive cards) in standard poker hand rankings.

What's the most important skill in poker?

Folding. Most beginner losses come from playing too many hands and not recognising when you're beaten. Strong starting hand selection and the discipline to fold are more valuable than reading tells or making elaborate bluffs.

How many players are in a standard Texas Hold'em game?

Texas Hold'em can be played with 2–10 players. Head-to-head (2-player, or "heads up") is a common format on Shitbox Shuffle because it's fast-paced and naturally suited to the 1-on-1 video match format.
