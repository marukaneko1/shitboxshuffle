How to Make Friends Online as an Adult in 2026

Studies consistently show that adults are lonelier than any previous generation—more connected digitally, less connected interpersonally. Making friends as an adult is genuinely harder than it was at 15, but the research is clear on what works and what doesn't. This is the practical guide.

Why adult friendships are harder to form

The difficulty is structural, not personal. Sociologist Rebecca Adams, whose research is the most frequently cited on this topic, identifies three conditions that friendship formation requires:

  1. Proximity: Physical or contextual closeness—being near the same people repeatedly.
  2. Repeated unplanned interaction: Bumping into each other, not just scheduled meetings. Spontaneity builds the casual familiarity that deepens into friendship.
  3. A setting where people feel safe to be vulnerable: School and college create this naturally. Adult professional and social life often doesn't.

Adult life erodes all three. Remote work removes proximity. Busy schedules replace spontaneous encounters with planned ones. The social self-protection that comes with age reduces vulnerability. The result is what millions of adults experience: plenty of acquaintances, not enough real friends.

Platforms that work (and what they're suited for)

Different platforms serve different parts of the friendship-formation puzzle:

  • Interest-based communities (Reddit, Discord, niche forums): Excellent for finding people with shared values and passions. Weak on spontaneous interaction—you control when and whether you engage, reducing the "repeated unplanned" element.
  • Multiplayer games (online games with chat): Strong on repeated interaction and shared experience. Friendship formation happens naturally around play. Weak on face-to-face connection, which accelerates depth.
  • Video-first platforms (random video chat, video game nights): Camera presence significantly accelerates rapport compared to text-only interaction. The face-to-face element creates the vulnerability condition that written platforms often don't.
  • Social apps (Bumble BFF, Meetup): Explicitly designed for adult friendship formation. Good for intentional connection but require both parties to be actively seeking friendship, which is a narrower pool.

The gamified approach to meeting people

Adding a game to a social interaction changes the dynamic fundamentally. Games do several things that improve social connection:

  • They remove the pressure of being "interesting enough" as a person—the game provides the activity
  • They create shared emotional experiences (winning, losing, close calls) that raw conversation rarely produces in a first meeting
  • They provide natural banter material without requiring wit or charm on demand
  • Competition creates the mild stress that, paradoxically, accelerates bonding (people who go through challenging experiences together connect faster)

This is the core insight behind gaming communities being disproportionately good at producing friendships—and why video + games is a particularly effective combination for adults trying to meet people.

Shared activity beats shared smalltalk

"What do you do for fun?" is a less effective connection path than actually doing something fun together. Research on what creates closeness consistently shows that collaborative or competitive activity generates more emotional connection per unit of time than conversation alone.

The practical implication: rather than looking for platforms to "chat" on, look for platforms where you're doing something alongside the chat. The something provides the connective tissue.

Consistency matters more than intensity

Adult friendships don't form from a single great interaction—they form from repeated exposure over time. The research on friendship timelines suggests:

  • 50 hours of interaction → casual friend
  • 90 hours → close friend
  • 200+ hours → intimate friendship

These numbers aren't prescriptions—they're averages across a large sample. The implication is that a brief intense connection rarely becomes a lasting friendship without regular, ongoing contact. Platforms that facilitate recurring interactions (game nights, return matches, community threads) support this better than one-off encounters.

Converting online to lasting friendship

Moving an online connection from "person I occasionally see on a platform" to "actual friend" requires a few deliberate steps:

  • Exchange contact outside the platform. Platform-dependent relationships are fragile—one of you leaves and it ends. A separate contact (Discord, text, email) creates independence from the platform.
  • Be the one who reaches out. Most online connections drift because neither person makes the first explicit "hey, let's keep this going" move. Being that person has asymmetric upside—most people appreciate being contacted and respond positively.
  • Schedule the next thing. "We should do this again" is an intention, not a commitment. "Same time next week?" is a commitment.
  • Move toward video if you haven't already. Voice beats text, video beats voice, for relationship depth. Each format upgrade deepens the connection faster.

Safety in adult online social spaces

The caveats for meeting strangers online apply regardless of your intent:

  • Be thoughtful about what personal information you share before you've established baseline trust
  • Genuine friendship doesn't come with financial requests—be alert to connections that escalate quickly to requests for money or favours
  • Protect your primary contact details until you've had multiple interactions over time
  • Use platforms with active moderation and reporting tools

FAQ

Is it weird to try to make friends online as an adult?

Not anymore. Adult loneliness is a documented public health issue. Online-origin friendships have been normalised by gaming, pandemic-era remote socialisation, and the general shift of social life to digital contexts. The stigma around "online friends" has largely collapsed, and the friendships themselves are real.

What's the fastest way to make friends online?

Play games with the same people repeatedly. The research is clear that shared activity + repeated exposure + mild challenge = faster friendship formation than any conversation-first approach. Find a game community, show up consistently, and the friendships develop as a byproduct.

How do online friendships compare to in-person ones?

Research suggests online friendships can reach the same depth as in-person ones, particularly when they include video communication and shared activity. They tend to develop more slowly without the physical environment cues that accelerate in-person connection, but the potential for depth is equivalent.
