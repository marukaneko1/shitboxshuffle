How blackjack works (quick rules)

You're dealt two cards. The goal is to get closer to 21 than the dealer without going over ("busting"). Face cards are worth 10. Aces are worth 1 or 11.

Your options each turn:

  • Hit: Take another card.
  • Stand: Keep your current hand.
  • Double down: Double your bet and receive exactly one more card.
  • Split: If your two cards are a pair, split them into two separate hands (requires matching your original bet).
  • Surrender (when available): Fold and recover half your bet.

The dealer plays by fixed rules: hit on 16 or below, stand on 17 or above. You have choices the dealer doesn't—that's where strategy lives.

The basic strategy chart

Basic strategy was first developed in the 1950s by mathematicians using early computer simulations. It maps every combination of your hand total vs. the dealer's upcard to the statistically optimal action.

The chart is divided into three sections:

  1. Hard totals (hands without an ace, or where the ace must count as 1)
  2. Soft totals (hands with an ace counting as 11)
  3. Pair splits (when both cards are the same value)

You don't need to memorize the chart instantly. The key insight is that most decisions are obvious once you understand the underlying principle: the dealer's upcard tells you how likely the dealer is to bust. When the dealer shows a 4, 5, or 6, they're vulnerable—you play conservatively and let them bust. When the dealer shows a 9, 10, or Ace, they're strong—you need to take more risk.

Hard totals (memorise these first)

  • 8 or less: Always hit.
  • 9: Double if dealer shows 3–6. Otherwise hit.
  • 10: Double if dealer shows 2–9. Hit against 10 or Ace.
  • 11: Double if dealer shows 2–10. Hit against dealer Ace.
  • 12: Stand if dealer shows 4–6. Hit everything else.
  • 13–16: Stand if dealer shows 2–6. Hit if dealer shows 7 or higher.
  • 17 or more: Always stand.

The 16-vs-10 situation (hit or stand on hard 16 when dealer shows a 10) is the most debated hand. Basic strategy says hit. It's a losing hand either way—hitting gives marginally better expected value than standing.

Soft totals (Ace + another card)

Soft hands are flexible because the Ace can count as 1 or 11. This flexibility means you can often double or hit more aggressively without busting.

  • Soft 13–14 (A+2, A+3): Double if dealer shows 5–6. Otherwise hit.
  • Soft 15–16 (A+4, A+5): Double if dealer shows 4–6. Otherwise hit.
  • Soft 17 (A+6): Double if dealer shows 3–6. Otherwise hit.
  • Soft 18 (A+7): Stand if dealer shows 2, 7, or 8. Double if dealer shows 3–6. Hit if dealer shows 9, 10, or Ace.
  • Soft 19 or 20: Always stand.

When to split pairs

  • Always split: Aces and 8s. Aces together are weak (combined 12); split gives two chances at blackjack. 8s together make 16—the worst hand in blackjack; split gives two fighting chances.
  • Never split: 10s (combined 20 is excellent) and 5s (combined 10 is strong—double instead).
  • Split 2s, 3s, 7s: Against dealer 2–7. Otherwise hit.
  • Split 4s: Only against dealer 5 or 6. Otherwise hit.
  • Split 6s: Against dealer 2–6. Otherwise hit.
  • Split 9s: Against dealer 2–6 and 8–9. Stand against dealer 7, 10, or Ace.

When to double down

Doubling down is powerful when you're likely to make a strong hand and the dealer is weak. The rules are baked into the hard and soft total guidelines above, but the pattern to remember:

  • Hard 9, 10, and 11 are your best doubling hands—you're likely to land a 10-value card and end up with 19, 20, or 21.
  • Soft hands from 13–18 can be doubled against specific dealer upcards (4–6 mostly) because the dealer is vulnerable and you have downside protection from the flexible Ace.
  • Never double on hard 12 or higher—you risk busting, and the dealer hasn't given you a reason to escalate.

Common beginner mistakes

  1. Standing on 16 against a dealer 10. Feels safe (you might not bust), but the dealer is likely at 20. You need to take the risk.
  2. Splitting 10s. A combined 20 wins most of the time. Don't break it up chasing two good hands.
  3. Not doubling 11 against dealer 6. This is one of the highest expected-value plays in the game. Players who skip it are leaving value on the table consistently.
  4. Taking insurance. Insurance is a side bet on the dealer having a 10-value hole card. The math doesn't support it at standard odds. Skip it.
  5. Playing hunches over strategy. "I feel like I'll bust" or "I have a feeling" are not inputs in the strategy chart. The chart was built on tens of millions of simulated hands. Your feeling wasn't.

FAQ

Does basic strategy guarantee you win at blackjack?

No. Basic strategy minimises the house edge over the long run—to below 0.5% in standard rule sets. In any single session, luck plays a significant role. Basic strategy improves your expected outcome but doesn't eliminate variance.

Is card counting useful in online or video blackjack?

Card counting requires tracking the ratio of high to low cards across a large number of hands from a single deck or shoe. It's ineffective in most online implementations where decks are reshuffled frequently. Focus on basic strategy for session play.

What's the best first card to be dealt?

An Ace. It creates a soft hand with maximum flexibility. The second-best opening card depends on context, but any 10-value card + Ace (blackjack) is the best two-card combination in the game.
