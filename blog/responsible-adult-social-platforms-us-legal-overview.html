Responsible Adult Social Platforms: US Legal Overview

A high-level, non-technical overview of the US legal framework that applies to adult social and video platforms. Useful context for understanding why platforms make the design choices they do.

Federal law basics

Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act

Section 230 provides online platforms with limited immunity from liability for user-generated content. Platforms are generally not treated as publishers of their users' speech, which means they can't be sued for what users say or do on the platform (with specific exceptions including CSAM).

This protection is not unlimited: platforms that "develop" harmful content (more than just hosting it) can lose Section 230 protection. The scope of what constitutes "developing" content is actively litigated.

COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act)

COPPA requires verifiable parental consent before collecting personal data from children under 13. Platforms directed at children face the strongest requirements. General-audience platforms that happen to be used by children face softer requirements but must have COPPA-compliant practices if they have actual knowledge that they're collecting data from children under 13.

CAN-SPAM Act

Applies to email marketing communications. Platforms that send marketing emails must comply with opt-out, labeling, and contact information requirements. Relatively low compliance burden for most digital platforms.

State-level developments

State law is where the most active development is occurring for adult and social platforms:

  • Age verification laws: Multiple states (Texas, Louisiana, Arkansas, Virginia, others) have passed or are pursuing laws requiring age verification for social media platforms and/or adult content platforms. These vary significantly in scope and have faced constitutional challenges.
  • Minor protection laws: Several states have enacted laws specifically requiring social media platforms to obtain parental consent for minor users, apply age-appropriate design requirements, or restrict certain features for minors.
  • Online gaming and wagering: Skill game regulation varies by state. Some states explicitly permit skill-based wagering platforms; others have ambiguous or prohibitive frameworks. Operating in multiple states requires state-specific legal review.
  • Privacy laws (CCPA and equivalents): California's Consumer Privacy Act and similar laws in Colorado, Virginia, and others impose data handling, disclosure, and opt-out requirements that apply to platforms with users in those states.

Age verification requirements

For adult social platforms specifically:

  • No single federal law currently requires age verification for all online social platforms
  • State laws vary significantly—some require verification, some require parental consent for minors, some have no specific requirements
  • Platforms that host adult content have stronger obligations in multiple states
  • Platforms with wagering activities face additional verification requirements that vary by state
  • The trend is clearly toward stronger verification requirements as legislation catches up to platform reality

Content moderation expectations

US law doesn't generally mandate specific content moderation approaches (except for CSAM, which must be reported to NCMEC). However:

  • FTC consumer protection authority covers platforms that engage in deceptive or unfair practices, which can include failing to address safety issues they've been made aware of
  • Advertising standards apply to platforms that sell ads or make marketing representations about safety
  • CSAM reporting is a hard legal requirement—platforms must report discovered child sexual abuse material to the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children
  • Some states are moving toward affirmative moderation requirements for platforms with minor users

Terms of Service requirements

Platforms are generally free to set their own Terms of Service, but:

  • TOS provisions that violate consumer protection law (deceptive provisions, hidden fees) can be challenged by the FTC or state AGs
  • Mandatory arbitration clauses have faced challenges in several states
  • Data handling provisions must comply with applicable privacy laws
  • Age restrictions must be genuinely enforced to have legal protective value—a TOS "18+ only" clause without verification doesn't protect a platform from liability if it actually serves minors

Payment and wagering adjacency

This is a complex area where platforms with token systems and wagering features need specific legal review:

  • Whether a token-based wagering system constitutes gambling under applicable state law is highly fact-specific
  • The predominant purpose test (is the outcome skill-based or chance-based?) is the most common analytical framework, but application varies by state
  • Payment processing requirements impose their own compliance obligations (KYC for high-volume transactions, reporting requirements, etc.)
  • Anti-money laundering considerations apply to platforms that handle significant token volumes

This area is not suitable for general overview guidance. Any platform with wagering-adjacent features requires qualified legal counsel familiar with online gaming law in the specific states where they operate.
